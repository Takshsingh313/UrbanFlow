Nagel-Schreckenberg Model

A theoretical definition for the stochastic cellular automata used in this simulation.

Abstract

The Nagel-Schreckenberg (NaSch) model is a discrete model for traffic flow simulation. Its capability to reproduce traffic jams without external bottlenecks makes it a valuable tool in urban dynamics research.

Ruleset Definition

The evolution of the system from time $t$ to $t+1$ is determined by the following four steps, applied parallelly to all vehicles:

1. Acceleration

$$v_i \leftarrow \min(v_i + 1, v_{max})$$

Unless they are already at maximum speed, all drivers will attempt to accelerate by one unit of velocity. This represents the natural desire to drive faster.

2. Deceleration (Safety)

$$v_i \leftarrow \min(v_i, d_i)$$

If the distance $d_i$ to the vehicle in front is smaller than the current velocity, the driver must brake to avoid a collision. The velocity is reduced to match the empty space ahead.

3. Randomization

$$v_i \leftarrow \max(v_i - 1, 0) \quad \text{with probability } p$$

This crucial step introduces stochasticity. It models human factors such as over-reaction to braking lights, distraction, or hesitation. This noise is what causes phantom traffic jams.

4. Position Update

$$x_i \leftarrow x_i + v_i$$

Finally, the vehicle moves forward by its new velocity $v_i$.
